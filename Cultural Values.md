# Schwartz’s Cultural Values Theory

> *“Values are the motivational goals that guide our choices — the shared compass of cultures and individuals alike.”*  
> — Shalom H. Schwartz

---

## Overview

**Shalom Schwartz’s Theory of Basic Human Values** is one of the most influential frameworks for understanding **universal human values** and how they vary **across individuals and cultures**.  

It identifies a set of **fundamental motivational domains** that shape how people **perceive, decide, and act**, offering a **map of human motivation** that links psychology, culture, and ethics.

For an organization like **Life Itself**, Schwartz’s work provides a **diagnostic lens** for understanding how **cultural value systems evolve**, and how to **design communities and institutions** that balance competing priorities such as self-expression, social order, and care for the whole.

---

## Core Ideas

### 1. Values as Universal Motivational Goals

Schwartz defines values as:
> “Desirable, trans-situational goals, varying in importance, that serve as guiding principles in people’s lives.”

They are **not behaviors** themselves, but **motivational orientations** underlying behavior — the *why* behind the *what*.

Values:
- Transcend specific situations (e.g. honesty matters across contexts)
- Serve as standards for judgment and choice
- Are ordered by relative importance (value hierarchies differ by person/culture)

---

### 2. The Ten Basic Human Values

Schwartz identified **ten universal value types**, each reflecting a distinct motivational goal.  
They’re organized in a **circumplex structure**, showing dynamic tensions and compatibilities.

| Value Type | Core Motivation | Example Expressions |
|-------------|----------------|---------------------|
| **Self-Direction** | Independent thought and action | Creativity, freedom, choosing own goals |
| **Stimulation** | Excitement, novelty, challenge | Adventure, risk-taking |
| **Hedonism** | Pleasure, sensuous gratification | Enjoying life, self-indulgence |
| **Achievement** | Personal success, competence | Ambition, capability |
| **Power** | Social status, control over others/resources | Wealth, authority |
| **Security** | Safety, stability, harmony | National security, social order |
| **Conformity** | Restraint of disruptive impulses | Obedience, self-discipline |
| **Tradition** | Respect for customs, ideas from religion or culture | Humility, devotion |
| **Benevolence** | Preservation/enhancement of others’ welfare | Caring, loyalty |
| **Universalism** | Understanding, tolerance, protection for all | Social justice, environmentalism |

---

### 3. The Circular Structure of Values

The **circular map** (circumplex) reveals that values are **organized by motivational conflict and compatibility**.

- **Adjacent values** share compatible motivations (e.g. Benevolence ↔ Universalism)
- **Opposite values** are often in tension (e.g. Power ↔ Universalism)

![cultural_values_circle](/assets/motivational_types_of_values.png)

This circular dynamic represents **trade-offs** individuals and cultures must navigate:
- **Self-transcendence ↔ Self-enhancement**
- **Openness to change ↔ Conservation**



---

### 4. Cultural-Level Value Dimensions

Schwartz extended his model to the **cultural level**, identifying how entire societies emphasize different value clusters:

| Dimension | Description | Cultural Emphasis |
|------------|--------------|-------------------|
| **Embeddedness ↔ Autonomy** | Collective order vs. individual freedom | East Asia vs. Western Europe |
| **Hierarchy ↔ Egalitarianism** | Legitimated inequality vs. moral equality | Traditional vs. democratic cultures |
| **Mastery ↔ Harmony** | Control over environment vs. fitting into nature | Industrialized vs. ecological cultures |

> These cultural value dimensions shape **social institutions, governance models, and worldviews** — linking to the paradigm discussions in *Life Itself*’s work.

---

## 5. Tensions and Balance

The strength of Schwartz’s framework lies in recognizing that **values coexist in dynamic tension**.  
Healthy individuals and cultures balance across the circle rather than collapsing into one pole.

For instance:

| Tension | Individual Level | Cultural / Organizational Level |
|----------|------------------|---------------------------------|
| **Openness vs. Conservation** | Innovation vs. stability | Experimentation vs. tradition |
| **Self-Transcendence vs. Self-Enhancement** | Altruism vs. ambition | Community vs. competition |

Organizations like **Life Itself** might seek a **“meta-balance”** — cultivating creative openness and universal care while maintaining coherence and continuity.

## 6. Relevance to Life Itself

**Life Itself**’s mission can be seen as a **value synthesis project**:  
- Integrating *Self-Transcendence* (universalism, benevolence) with *Self-Direction* (freedom, creativity)  
- Emphasizing *Harmony over Mastery*, aligned with ecological and spiritual regeneration  
- Creating communities that embody *Egalitarianism and Embeddedness* simultaneously — valuing both autonomy and belonging  

> In this light, Life Itself can be seen as an *evolutionary experiment in cultural value reconfiguration* — exploring how new forms of life and work embody a more integrated value ecology.

---

## 7. Implications for Cultural Change

Cultural evolution (as seen in the earlier page) often unfolds as a **shift in dominant value orientations**:
- From *security and conformity* → to *self-direction and universalism*
- From *mastery over nature* → to *harmony with nature*
- From *hierarchy* → to *egalitarian and participatory systems*

This theory thus offers a **map of moral and motivational evolution** — a way to understand what it means for a culture to “develop” or “mature” in consciousness.
